Definition

Price-to-earnings ratio or P/E of a company measures its current share price relative to its per-share earnings (EPS). Source: Investopedia

$$ P/E \,ratio = {Share \,price \over Earnings \,per \,share} $$

Significance

Let’s calculate P/E ratio of a fictional company Avi Corp. which is trading currently at $100 per share. Their EPS (Earnings per-share) is $20, making their P/E ratio = 100⁄20 = 5.

This can be interpreted as - “investors are willing to pay $5 for every $1 of company’s earnings”

==P/E ratio is a measure of expected earnings.==

Practical use

Valuation

  1. Higher P/E ratio = Either company’s stock is over-valued or investor’s expect growth in future because of company’s current investments in their product or otherwise.
  2. Lower P/E ratio = Either company’s stock is under-valued or they are already established as an industry leader and have little to no room to grow further.

Comparison

P/E ratio in itself is not a helpful indicator of a stock’s performance, but when compared with:

  • Other stocks in the same industry goup
  • Benchmark index (like S&P 500)
  • Historical P/E range of the same stock

it can reveal whether a stock is undervalued or overvalued.

Risk evaluation

Higher P/E ratio = Growth Stocks = High Risk

Lower P/E ratio = Value Stocks = Low Risk

Example

  • S&P P/E ratio for Q1 2020 = 22.22
  • SQUARE (SQ) P/E ratio (TTM) = 301.53
  • IT Services P/E ratio (Last Year GAAP Actual) = 54.58

I think it is safe to deduce that:

  1. SQUARE is a growth stock (currently trading at $ 175.34 as of 10/06/2020 4:10 PM)
  2. It is “riskier” to invest in Square at this price if you do not fully understand their growth plans, opportunities and roadmaps.
  3. It is also safe to say that this is an “overvalued” stock.

Variants of P/E Ratio

When calculating P/E ratio, numerator (Price of stock) is pretty straightforward metric but Earnings per share is dependent on time dimension (is it EPS of current month, quarter, year ?).

Three common variants to calculate P/E ratio look at denominator in different ways as discussed below.

Trailing Twelve Month (TTM) Earnings

Name is a dead giveaway here, you calculate Earnings or profits the company has made over the last 12 months. If you look at this number in October 2020 it will reflect the total earnings since October of previous year (October 2019 - September 2020)

$$ EPS = {Earnings \over Outstanding shares} $$

Earnings or profit = Revenue - Operating expenses Earnings can be easily found in the income statement of a company (often released every quarter) under the heading - Net Income

Outstanding Shares are also reported on income statement (so is EPS).

Forward Earnings

Forwards earnings are an estimate of a company’s earnings for upcoming periods. This at best is a guestimate and should be treated as such. It is usually calculated by analysts with the help of a company’s management providing earnings guidance.

Continuing our example of Square (SQ), MorningStar has its current Price/Forward Earnings ratio to be 151.52 and 5-Yr ratio to be 118.66.

Shiller P/E Ratio

Shiller P/E is calculated by dividing the price by the average earnings over the past 10 years adjusted for inflation.

I have personally not seen this in much use.

Limitations

Goes without saying that this is not the “only” metric you should see before deciding to invest in a stock. Consider following limitations of P/E ratio as a metric:

  1. Volatile market prices, we saw this in case of Tesla (TSLA) recently where the stock prices kept rising (still is). This can throw off P/E ratio in the short term.
  2. Earnings in the past is not a guarantee for earnings in the future. Forward Earnings tries to address this but its based on Wall Street analysts assumptions.
  3. It doesn’t capture company’s growth prospects. Buying a stock with higher P/E ratio might be a wise thing to do if you somehow know that earnings will grow in future bringing P/E back down to a lower level. But Earnings growth is not included in P/E ratio. ==Note: PEG Ratio does include it, more on it in a separate post==

Tools

You can find P/E ratio in any finance website which tracks stock prices (Yahoo, Google) or in the tool you use for investing (Robinhood, Fidelity, Vanguard). Here is a kicker, I found the numbers for the same stock differing at the same time

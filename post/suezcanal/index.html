Suez Canal in the time of COVID-19

2020-05-12
explanation
548 words 3 mins read

Contents

I doubt if any individual or industry on this planet is unaffected by the Coronavirus pandemic (hence a pandemic duh!). In this post I wanted to share some interesting facts I learned by following the news trail about oil prices.

Let’s start with what happened to oil prices in April 2020. A little background on Oil trading is necessary to grasp the full picture, so here we go:

Oil is traded on its future price. So if we are in the month of April, we can trade a futures contract to buy or sell Crude oil in a future month. Last trading day is defined as the third business day before the 25th calendar day of the month preceding the delivery month.

In April 2020, last trading day was 21st, which means if you were a trader with a buy futures contract for the month of May 2020, you were suppose to take delivery of at a minimum 1,000 U.S. barrels of oil (1 contract size = 1,000 U.S. barrels or 42,000 gallons) in May 2020.

With world population in lockdown, airlines not leaving their hangars, the demand for crude oil has plummeted all around the world. And all the usual buyers - governments, airlines, refineries etc. are at their peak capacity, storage wise.

This created a once in a lifetime situation where sellers were ready to pay a buyer to take oil from them and store it lest they will have it delivered to them.

This is why WTI Crude oil 1 futures contract was trading at negative $4.47 per barrel on 21st April 2020.

Now imagine one industry which is slightly less effected by this pandemic - the shipping industry, goods are still moving across continents (although less, but still). On top of that your mode of transport relies heavily on oil prices (which are record low).

This is where things get interesting (and the mystery unravels about the title of this post). Remember how we sometimes avoid toll because we don’t care if it takes us longer to reach our destination (because fuel is cheap and we have time to kill). Well shipping industry is choosing that option too, instead of going through Suez canal they are choosing to go all the way around Africa to reach Europe and North America.

And it is estimated that while the longer route will add more time to reach destination, it will save the shipping companies as much as $235,000 per voyage.2

To cope with this, Suez canal authority has introduced fee cuts which will last through to the end of June 3. Interesting times.
